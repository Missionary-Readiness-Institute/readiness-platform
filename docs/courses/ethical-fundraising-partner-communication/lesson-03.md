# Lesson 03: Managing Restricted Gifts vs. Unrestricted Gifts

## Lesson overview

Every gift must have a source, purpose, approval path, and documentation. This lesson helps learners manage restricted and unrestricted donations ethically.

## Key idea

Money is only ethical when the giver’s intent is clear and the organization’s response is documented.

## Why this matters

Unclear gift handling damages trust, harms donors, and makes the ministry vulnerable to misuse.

## Field context

This applies to cash gifts, large unmarked donations, project-specific support, and in-kind contributions.

## Learner role

The learner should ask clarifying questions, record donor intent, and follow the organization’s gift acceptance policy.

## Team role

Teams should protect restricted gifts from being used for unintended purposes and track each gift’s approval path.

## Preparation

- Review existing gift handling practices.
- Identify who approves restricted gifts.
- Draft clear documentation steps.

## Step 1: Classify gifts

Determine whether each gift is unrestricted, project-restricted, time-restricted, or conditional.

## Step 2: Clarify donor intent

Ask donors to state their purpose in writing and confirm any ambiguities before accepting large or unusual gifts.

## Step 3: Document approvals

Record the approval process so restricted gifts are only used as intended.

## Common challenges

Large unmarked gifts can create pressure to spend quickly. The right response is to pause, clarify, and document.

## Practical example

A donor offers a large gift without specifying a project. The team asks for written direction, records the gift, and only releases funds after approval.

## Reflection questions

1. What questions will you ask when a donor gives a restricted gift?
2. How will you document donor intent?
3. Who should approve the release of restricted funds?

## Summary

Managing restricted and unrestricted gifts is foundational for ethical fundraising.
